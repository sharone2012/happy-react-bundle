Soil Science Brief — Ultisols (Podsolik Merah Kuning)
Classification & Distribution
  • Order: Ultisols — ancient, deeply weathered tropical clays; low-activity clay mineralogy (kaolinite dominant)
  • 24% of Indonesian palm plantation land; primary CFI target market
  • pH: 4.2–5.0 — strongly to very strongly acidic; Al³⁺ solubility exceeds phytotoxic threshold below pH 4.5
  • Base saturation: <35%; most exchangeable Ca, Mg, K lost to millennia of leaching
Primary Fertility Constraints
  • P fixation: 65–70% of applied TSP immobilised by Al/Fe compounds within hours of application — unavailable to palm regardless of dose
  • N leaching: 35% of applied urea-N lost below the root zone before palm uptake
  • CEC: 4–8 cmol/kg — almost entirely OM-dependent; collapses as organic matter declines under conventional management
  • K leaching: 20% — moderate, but worsens as CEC degrades; compounding cycle
CFI Response Mechanism
  • Phosphate-solubilising bacteria (Bacillus megaterium, Aspergillus niger) dissolve Al–P and Fe–P mineral bonds — bypasses fixation pathway entirely
  • Humate fraction (CFI frass) creates new cation exchange sites from Season 1 — compounding CEC recovery
  • Lignin + chitin fractions shift F:B ratio from bacterial-dominated (0.10–0.30) toward fungal-dominated (>0.50) — improves aggregate stability and Ganoderma suppression
  • AMF colonisation rises as soil OC exceeds 2% threshold — extends effective P uptake radius by up to 15 cm
Application Protocol & Financial Case
  • Standard rate: 2 t/ha/yr BF+ or 15–20 t/ha/yr Compost+ (wet weight)
  • Liming requirement eliminated after Year 2 as soil pH stabilises through humate buffering
  • Combined NPK waste on synthetic programme: ~$76/ha/yr (P fixation $31 + N leach $30 + K leach $15)
  • CFI recovers an estimated $47/ha/yr of this loss at standard dose; improving compounding as soil biology establishes

Cation Exchange Capacity Trajectory — Year 0 Through Year 5 With CFI
CEC measures the soil's ability to hold and slowly release nutrients; higher CEC means more nutrients retained against leaching, fewer split applications needed, and more stable palm nutrition year-round.
Without CFI, Ultisol CEC slowly declines as organic matter is consumed. With CFI application, CEC rises from ~6 cmol/kg at Year 0 to an estimated ~14 cmol/kg by Year 5 — approaching Inceptisol levels — as humate fractions accumulate in the soil matrix.
Soil Organic Carbon Trajectory — Building Carbon Sequestration Over 5 Years
Soil organic carbon is the foundation of all biological and chemical fertility — it feeds microbes, builds CEC, improves water retention, and sequesters atmospheric carbon into stable long-term soil pools.
Conventional management depletes Ultisol SOC from an already-low 1.0% toward 0.75% over five years. CFI application reverses this trajectory, with the recalcitrant lignin (ADL) fraction providing stable carbon input resistant to decomposition for decades.
Soil Organic Matter Accumulation — Annual Humus Formation Rate

Category 3 — Soil Biology: Microbes, Fungi And Disease Suppression 3 charts · MBC, AMF colonisation, F:B ratio · Ganoderma and nematode suppression ▼ Show Charts
Microbial Biomass Carbon — Biological Activity Recovery From Degraded Baseline
MBC is the leading indicator of long-term soil fertility — the mass of living microorganisms in the soil drives nutrient cycling, disease suppression, aggregate formation, and the biological weathering that releases minerals from rock particles over time.
Degraded Ultisol MBC (100–250 mg/kg) is approximately 25% of the target range, indicating a severely depleted biological community. CFI frass provides both the carbon substrate and the inoculant organisms to rebuild MBC toward 500–1,000 mg/kg over three to five seasons, restoring biological nutrient cycling that synthetic fertilisers entirely bypass.
Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi Colonisation — P Uptake Efficiency And Drought Resilience
AMF are the root extension network of oil palm — colonising root cells and extending fungal hyphae up to 15 cm into soil to intercept phosphorus, zinc, and water that root hairs cannot reach, in exchange for palm photosynthate.
Ultisol AMF colonisation at 5–15% is far below the 40–70% target, meaning palm roots are operating with a fraction of their potential nutrient uptake network. CFI application creates the organic carbon substrate that AMF require to proliferate, with colonisation improving progressively as soil carbon rises above the 2% threshold.
Fungal-To-Bacterial Ratio Trajectory — Shift Toward Long-Term Soil Structure
The F:B ratio indicates whether a soil's biology is shifting toward bacterial-dominated (fast nutrient cycling, structure breakdown) or fungal-dominated (slow cycling, stable aggregates, Ganoderma suppression) — higher is better for long-term palm estate productivity.
Degraded Ultisol F:B of 0.10–0.30 is strongly bacterial-dominated, associated with rapid organic matter loss and reduced Ganoderma suppression. CFI's lignin and chitin fractions selectively feed fungal communities, shifting the ratio toward 0.50–1.00 over five seasons, which correlates with natural Ganoderma disease pressure reduction of 30–50%.

Expanded Analysis Inceptisols (Kambisol / Gleisol) Largest Market By Area — 39%
Soil Science Brief — Inceptisols
Inceptisols are the best soils in the Indonesian palm system — covering 39% of plantation land, they are relatively young and moderately developed soils with genuine clay mineralogy that responds generously to organic matter inputs, the agricultural equivalent of a patient who is not yet sick but will thrive with good nutrition. Their pH of 4.0–5.0 still causes phosphorus fixation at 30% and moderate nitrogen leaching at 25%, but both problems are manageable compared to the catastrophic losses seen on Ultisols, Oxisols, or Spodosols. The critical number on Inceptisols is not the loss rate but the recovery rate: each 1% of soil organic matter added raises cation exchange capacity by 2.8 cmol/kg — the highest mineral CEC response of any soil type in the palm system, meaning that every tonne of CFI frass applied generates compounding fertility improvements that accumulate season after season in a virtuous cycle that no synthetic programme can replicate. Mycorrhizal fungi colonise Inceptisols readily once organic carbon rises above 2%, unlocking additional phosphorus from the soil's mineral reserve that has been fixed there for years, effectively recovering free nutrients that the estate has already paid for. The Inceptisol estate is not a problem to be solved; it is a performance platform waiting to be activated by the biological and organic inputs that its 2:1 clay mineralogy is chemically primed to respond to at scale.

Expanded Analysis Oxisols (Latosol / Ferralsol) Highest Individual CFI Impact Per Ha
Soil Science Brief — Oxisols
Oxisols represent the most extreme fertiliser challenge in the Indonesian palm system — their entire mineralogy is dominated by iron and aluminium oxides, the very compounds responsible for phosphorus fixation, and at 78% P fixation they render conventional TSP applications almost entirely ineffective from the moment the fertiliser contacts the soil surface. Think of it this way: a plantation manager applying triple superphosphate to an Oxisol is spending 78 cents of every dollar they invest in phosphorus on fertiliser that the soil chemistry will seize and lock away permanently. With a cation exchange capacity of just 3–6 cmol/kg and a base saturation below 20%, these soils cannot hold calcium, magnesium, or potassium in plant-available form, meaning that every significant rainstorm strips the fertiliser programme back toward zero. The only long-term solution to Oxisol fertility is organic matter — each 1% of soil organic matter added does not merely feed the plant directly, it physically creates new exchange sites in a soil that has almost none, and the phosphate-solubilising bacteria living in CFI frass provide the only practical pathway to phosphorus availability by dissolving the iron-aluminium bonds that hold 78 cents of every TSP dollar captive. A doubled CFI application rate on Oxisols is not an excessive recommendation — it is the minimum biological intervention required to move these soils from their current near-zero productivity baseline toward something approaching the agronomic performance that the original planting investment was designed to deliver.

Expanded Analysis Histosols — Peat Soils (Tanah Gambut) Peat Protocol — Special Rules Apply
Soil Science Brief — Histosols (Peat)
Histosols — peat soils — operate under a completely different set of rules from every other soil type in the palm system, and applying conventional fertiliser programmes to them is one of the most costly and agronomically counterproductive mistakes in Indonesian plantation management. With organic carbon already at 24.5% and pH at 3.8, the primary nitrogen challenge on peat is not leaching but immobilisation: the very high carbon-to-nitrogen ratio of peat — above 50 — means that the soil's microbial community consumes applied nitrogen before the palm root can intercept it, locking it into microbial biomass where it remains unavailable until that biomass dies and is mineralised, a process that takes months to years. Think of it as paying for nitrogen and having the soil microbes steal it before the palm sees a single kilogram. Potassium is the critical nutrient on peat — drainage systems flush 40% of applied potassium through the peat profile, and without sustained K supply palm bunches abort and yield crashes. Lime is strictly prohibited on Histosols because calcium carbonate triggers irreversible peat oxidation and subsidence of 2–3 cm per year. CFI's product on peat soils addresses the three endemic deficiencies simultaneously — calcium from the frass mineral fraction corrects pH without the oxidation risk of lime, the BSF cuticle delivers copper and zinc that are structurally absent from peat mineralogy, and the slow-release organic nitrogen fraction bypasses the immobilisation trap that defeats conventional urea on every peat estate in Indonesia.

Expanded Analysis Spodosols — Sandy Entisols (Podzol / BRIS) Lowest Fertility · Highest Transformation Potential
Soil Science Brief — Spodosols
Spodosols are the most structurally degraded soils in the Indonesian palm system — coastal and inland sands with almost zero clay content, a cation exchange capacity of just 2.0 cmol/kg, and nitrogen leaching losses of 50% that mean half of every kilogram of applied Urea is washed through the profile before a root hair has the opportunity to find it. Unlike every other Indonesian palm soil where phosphorus is locked by iron and aluminium, on Spodosols phosphorus behaves in the opposite direction — it leaches rather than fixes, moving rapidly through the sandy profile in the same way nitrogen does, which means the standard agronomic response of applying more TSP to overcome soil retention is doubly ineffective: the phosphorus does not stay long enough to be fixed, but also does not stay long enough to be taken up. Yields on Spodosols are structurally 31% below Ultisol baseline not because of a single limiting factor but because of compounding deficiencies across every fertility parameter simultaneously — the CEC is near zero so nutrients cannot be held, the biology is near zero so there is no microbial buffering, and the water holding capacity is so low that drought stress compounds every nutrient deficit into a yield crisis. CFI's humate fraction provides the only viable long-term intervention: each 1% of soil organic matter added to a Spodosol raises water holding capacity by 4 mm — the highest WHC response of any soil type in the system — and simultaneously raises CEC by 1.75 cmol/kg, transforming a soil that currently retains almost nothing into one that can hold a meaningful nutrient reserve.

Expanded Analysis Andisols — Volcanic Ash Soils (Tanah Vulkanik) Highest P Fixation 90% · Allophane Mineralogy
Soil Science Brief — Andisols
Andisols are volcanically derived soils found on the slopes and foothills of Indonesia's active volcanic arc, and they represent one of the great paradoxes in tropical soil science: simultaneously the most naturally fertile and the most phosphorus-challenged soils in the entire Indonesian palm system. Their allophane mineralogy — an amorphous aluminium silicate formed directly from volcanic glass — gives them a suite of exceptional physical properties that agronomists elsewhere dream about: high water holding capacity, low bulk density, excellent natural aggregate stability, and the highest cation exchange capacity response per unit of organic matter of any soil in the CFI system at 3.2 cmol/kg per 1% SOM. The problem, and it is a serious one, is phosphorus: allophane binds phosphate with extraordinary chemical affinity, fixing 90% of applied TSP into forms the palm root cannot reach within minutes of application — the highest fixation rate of any soil type in the system. Critically, this is a different fixation mechanism from Ultisol or Oxisol chemistry: allophane creates what soil chemists call inner-sphere surface complexes with phosphate ions, bonds that are more stable and harder to release than the iron-aluminium oxide bonds that dominate Ultisol and Oxisol P fixation. Calcium silicate (CaSiO₃) co-amendment at 2.5–4.5 t/ha is the confirmed intervention, physically displacing phosphate from allophane surfaces by competitive silicate sorption; CFI's chelated organic phosphorus compounds provide a parallel biological pathway that circumvents the fixation mechanism entirely rather than competing with it chemically, making the Andisol estate the most compelling combined inorganic-organic fertiliser case in the Indonesian palm sector.
